You don't remember falling asleep, which means your body simply made the decision without consulting you. You guess that somewhere around hour seventy it had apparently convened an internal committee, reached a unanimous verdict, and that was that. The cot beneath you is pre-[System] military surplus, made completely of just canvas and aluminum tubing, and obviously designed by someone who believed that comfort was a form of weakness and had very strong opinions about it.

You lie still and run a quiet internal check. Your mana is regenerating from having to recharge the monster detection system you have set up outside. Your body is... operational? Sure, we'll go with that. As for your mind... it's present. The grief is exactly where you left it, locked in the specific interior room you've been reinforcing for eight years, and the door is holding strong. Thankfully, you've become, through extensive and largely involuntary practice, very good at this particular maintenance task.

You listen to Callie's machine hum to your left, her tools clinking against the magic metal it's composed of. You take one more breath of the stale, but safe, recycled air.

The gear rack, full of salvaged armor and weapons, was your contribution to the sanctuary's infrastructure, assembling it during the first week when you needed something to do other thank thinking about how royally screwed the three of you are. It is, if you're being honest, the finest piece of non-magical construction in the room, which is either a point of pride or a fairly damning commentary on where a lot of your skill points went. You begin checking the clasps and tensions anyway, running through the routine you've been completing every morning for years because rituals are load-bearing, and you've learned not to dismantle the ones that work. Most of the armor is dulled or broken in a way that no amount of maintenance could ever fully correct, though it's still better than the best pre-[System] armor ever devised. It's kind of embarrassing in a way, considering your final [Class] evolution is called [legendary]Shield of Humanity[/legendary]. And though you do have a skill that allows you to fix small damage in your weapons and armor, you're no blacksmith.

The weapons here are in better shape, though.

You get up and cross the room.

The machine takes up most of the back wall, and it still does something weird to your chest and head every time you look at it. It's like a very particular variety of vertigo, but not the kind that comes from heights or spinning or falling. It's from looking at something that shouldn't exist, something that is so far beyond the limits of what the world should be capable of that your brain just doesn't know how to process it. Once upon a time, you'd have thought that it's what it must be like to look upon an eldritch horror, but having done that many times now, you know that it's not quite right.

Copper conduits spiral around an ironite-obsidian central housing. Dungeon cores from Tier 8 and 9 space-time instances are slotted into a lattice of [System]-generated materials the three of you had gathered for years, unable to imagine this is what they'd eventually be used for. It's every high-tier drop the three of you had managed to hoard, stripped down and reassembled into something that the [System] didn't even have a category for, and that by itself was quite a feat.

Behind a viewport of reinforced magesteel glass, at the center of all of it, a single point of light spins.

A singularity.

"Hey Callie," you say.

Callie doesn't look up, and you instantly recognize the expression she gets when she's very close to the edge of something great. She's focused to the point where the rest of the world, and even her own health, has been downgraded to background noise, filed under *deal with it later,* and left on read.

The dark rings under her eyes have deepend and evolved from exhaustion to something that looks more like a medical condition. Her hair is held back with a piece of wire, which you're pretty certain is one of the wires from your ward scrolls that she asked to borrow four days ago for *just a second.* 

You have known Callie Owens since your second year of college, which means you knew her before the [System], before the descent, before the long catastrophic sequence of events that transformed the two of you from people who argued about where the best place near campus to eat was into people who have watched the world end together and are now, improbably, preparing to actually do something about it. 

You knew her when she was *just* the most frighteningly capable person you'd ever met. Now the [System] has given her a Tier rating and a Legendary [Class] as a confirmation. But you already knew.

Two hours and ten minutes.

You let yourself hold that number for a moment without immediately stress-testing it, which is actually harder than it sounds. You've had eight years of learning that good news generally came with lots and lots of fine print. 

For instance, four months ago, the portal mages had sealed the last entrance to the surface. There were no gates or tunnels; just a mile of solid rock between the city of Solace and the horrors above. It was the last coalition of surving nations, built in the earth's hollow because there was nowhere else to go but down. Legions of geomancers sheared the rock asunder, architecture mages laid the streets and buildings, and the most powerful illusionists created a false sky on the ceiling so the survivors could look up at something other than stone in dispair.

Everyone dispaired, regardless. How could they not?

*page_break But it mostly worked, for all of four months.

For four months, as far as anyone knows, there has not been a single living human being on the surface. The monsters had done what monsters do when left entirely to their own devices: autonomously summoned more of their kind, fought and consumed each other to become more powerful and stranger, and evolved in directions that no one could have anticipated. They got smarter, and they eventually began looking for wherever humanity had tried to hide. 

It didn't take long for them to start looking down.

But in two hours and ten minutes, none of it will matter anymore. You find, carefully, that you almost believe it.
